# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Two segments who both draw on paper from photo references using a grid technique:

- **Beginners**: Learning to draw, want structure and encouragement. Use the app casually, at home, with basic materials (pencil, gouache). Need the app to be non-intimidating and guide them step by step.
- **Experienced artists**: Use grid transfer seriously as a technique. Want accuracy, efficiency, and support for professional materials (watercolor, oil, ink). Less hand-holding, more power.

Both users open the app at their desk or easel, phone propped next to their paper, working square-by-square. The interface is a tool they glance at while drawing — it must be instantly readable and never get in the way.

## Product Purpose

PictureArt helps people redraw any photo on paper by splitting it into a grid of manageable squares. Optionally applies AI style transfer (Stability AI) to simulate gouache, watercolor, oil, ink, etc. before splitting. Presents one square at a time for the user to copy manually. Tracks progress across sessions.

Success looks like: a beginner completing their first drawing and feeling proud; an experienced artist using the grid as a professional tool without friction.

## Brand Personality

Warm, encouraging, creative. Not loud — it should feel like a well-designed sketchbook companion. Three words: **focused, creative, encouraging**.

The app earns trust by being calm and purposeful. It celebrates the user's work, not itself.

## Anti-references

- **Children's coloring apps** (Colorfy, Painting by Numbers): Too playful, rainbow palette, cartoonish icons — not for serious artists
- **Corporate SaaS** (any enterprise dashboard): Cold blue tones, sterile layout, enterprise-gray — wrong emotional register
- **Complex creative editors** (Procreate, Photoshop): Cluttered toolbars, overwhelming options — this app does ONE thing well
- **Typical AI aesthetic 2024**: Cream/beige backgrounds, identical card grids, gradient text, glassmorphism, those tracked uppercase eyebrow labels — instant credibility killer with artists

## Design Principles

1. **One thing at a time.** Each screen has one job. The grid view shows progress. The square view shows the tile. Don't put everything everywhere.
2. **The art comes first.** The user's photo and tile images are the hero. The UI recedes so the artwork fills the screen.
3. **Calm confidence.** No bouncing animations, no loud colors fighting for attention. The interface is a steady hand.
4. **Earn delight through detail.** Small things: the haptic tap on a grid cell, the green checkmark appearing, the color swatches extracted from each tile. Not flashy, but satisfying.
5. **Works at a glance.** The user is drawing — they look up, need one piece of information, and look back down. Readability and contrast are non-negotiable.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- WCAG AA minimum (4.5:1 body text contrast)
- Support Dynamic Type for users who need larger text
- Reduced motion: all animations must have instant/crossfade fallback
- iOS native conventions (NavigationStack, sheet, swipeActions) so the interface is predictable for all iOS users
